Checks and Balances

Everyone watches everyone else

Congress Checks the President

  • Can override presidential vetoes (2/3 vote)
  • Controls all federal spending ("power of the purse")
  • Can impeach and remove the President
  • Senate confirms Cabinet and judges

President Checks Congress

  • Can veto any bill Congress passes
  • Proposes the annual budget
  • Enforces (or deprioritizes) laws
  • Commander-in-Chief of military

Courts Check Both

  • Can declare laws unconstitutional
  • Can strike down executive actions
  • Interpret what laws actually mean
  • Lifetime tenure = independence

The Federal Budget Process

Appropriations bills are how Congress exercises its constitutional "power of the purse."

üìä

February: President's Budget Request

The President submits a massive, detailed budget proposal to Congress‚Äîthousands of pages covering every agency, program, and dollar. However, this is just a "wish list."

The Constitution gives Congress, not the President, the "power of the purse." The President proposes; Congress disposes.

üìê

March-April: Budget Resolution

Congress is supposed to pass a "budget resolution" setting overall spending limits‚Äîhow much for defense, how much for domestic programs, etc.

The budget resolution is NOT a law‚Äîthe President doesn't sign it. It's an internal agreement between the House and Senate.

üí∞

April-September: Appropriations Process

This is where the real work happens. 12 subcommittees each write their spending bills, deciding exactly how much money goes to each program and agency.

Appropriations committees are considered the most powerful in Congress because they control the money.

üìÖ

October 1: Fiscal Year Deadline

The new fiscal year begins. If all 12 appropriations bills aren't signed into law by midnight on September 30, the government either shuts down OR operates under a Continuing Resolution.

Since 1977, Congress has passed all 12 bills on time only 4 times. CRs have become the norm.

‚öôÔ∏è

Throughout the Year: Budget Execution

Once funded, agencies spend the money according to what Congress appropriated. They cannot legally spend more than authorized or spend it on unauthorized purposes.

This is when contracts get signed, projects move forward, and the government actually operates.

‚ö†Ô∏è Reality Check

Since 1977, Congress has passed all 12 appropriations bills on time only 4 times. The Department of Defense has started the fiscal year under a Continuing Resolution in 11 of the last 12 years. This dysfunction is now the norm, not the exception.

Where We Are Now

The current state of federal budget dysfunction

FY 2025 (Oct 2024 ‚Äì Sept 2025)

Congress never passed real appropriations bills. Instead, the government operated under a full-year Continuing Resolution passed in March 2025‚Äîthe first time ever for the Pentagon. This meant spending stayed at FY 2024 levels with limited adjustments.

FY 2026 (Oct 2025 ‚Äì Sept 2026)

Congress couldn't agree again. The government shut down on October 1, 2025 for 43 days‚Äîthe longest in U.S. history. About 900,000 federal employees were furloughed and 2 million more worked without pay.

The shutdown ended November 12, 2025 with a CR funding the government through January 30, 2026.
